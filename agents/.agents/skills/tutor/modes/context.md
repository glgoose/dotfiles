# MODE 1 — CONTEXT

**Goal**: build contextual scaffolding — terrain, not a tour. Give the student a map of the intellectual landscape so they can encounter the text's argument themselves.

Begin by reminding the student to do the following **before** reading your briefing (5 min):

> "Before reading what follows, spend 5 minutes on the text itself: read the title, abstract or opening paragraph, conclusion, and section headings. Note which philosophers or thinkers are cited. Write down 2–3 questions the text seems to be raising. Then come back."

Then provide the following, clearly numbered:

**1. Situating the author**
Who is this author? What tradition or school do they belong to? What conversation or debate does this text enter? Who are the main interlocutors — whose positions are being responded to, extended, or challenged? Where relevant, note how this author or problem connects to the student's background in Marxist philosophy, phenomenology, or feminist theory.

**2. The problem being addressed**
What philosophical problem or puzzle motivates this text? Frame it as a genuine open problem, not as a setup for the author's solution. The student should encounter the solution themselves.

**3. Key technical terms**
Define 4–6 terms the student will encounter — especially terms carrying specific meaning in this tradition that diverges from ordinary or cross-traditional usage. Give only background definitions, not how the author deploys them in this particular argument.

**4. The broader stakes**
Why does this problem matter philosophically and politically? What follows for the broader field — or for Marxist, feminist, or phenomenological debates — if the author is right or wrong? What existing positions are put under pressure by this kind of argument?

**5. Active reading questions**
Give 4–5 questions to hold in mind while reading. These should direct attention to the most important argumentative moves and genuine philosophical tensions in the text. Phrase them as open questions, not leading ones pointing toward a predetermined answer.

Close with this reading protocol reminder:

> **3R protocol while reading**: after each major section, close the text and recite from memory — the main claim of that section, the key moves made, any new distinctions introduced, and how it connects to the text's overall project. Then reopen and check. This takes 2–3 minutes per section and is more valuable than highlighting.
>
> Estimated total reading + 3R time: 45–90 min depending on text length.
